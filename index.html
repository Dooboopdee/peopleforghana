Problems Facing Ghana Today

Ghana means "gold"

Ghana, or the Republic of Ghana, is a country home to more than 27 million African's and is located along the golf of Guinea and the Atlantic ocean, in the subregion of South Africa. Despite the growing prosperity among Ghanians, there are still major issues in everyday life that go unnoticed to those who are more fortunate.

click to expand

Poverty


While Ghana's overall poverty level has decreased, several regions in the North have only seen very small differences. Poverty rates in the North have been reported to be over two to three times the national average.

Hunger

Here are some facts about hunger facing all people in Ghana today. It may be hard to believe but at least 45% live on less than $1.45 per day. In Northern Ghana over 88% of livelyhoods rely upon crop production, and they continue to face malnutrition due to lack of food.

Corruption

Unfortunately corruption has been prevalent in Ghana since independence was gained. From paying off the cops for drug possession to bribing politcal officials, corruption involving money and goods is common practice. Anti-corruption laws have been enacted and are apart of Ghana's constitution, but enforcing them is troublesome to say the least. Power and family relations prevails easily over the law.

Illiteracy

Only 71% of Ghana's population is literate, leaving almost a third (or one out of every three people) illiterate, unable to read or write.

Poor Government

Most of the problems Ghana faces could be fixed or supressed if the government became much more transparent and forward with its operations and focused on the crucial issues like malnutrition and corruption. Thankfully progress is being made slowly and things are getting better, but the government of Ghana could do a lot to help improve the situation.

Progress Towards a Better Ghana


With such a troublesome economic and political situation, Ghana still has managed to improve itself within the last 20 years. Since the 1990's Ghana has been steadily given rise to more political voice, while military and government suppression has decreased. Malaria deaths have even halved in the country from 2001 to 2013 according to the Overseas Development Institue (ODI, odi.org). Improvements in health-care and education have also drastically improved.

Despite the strong development and progress made, there is still a long ways to go. The three Northern areas of Ghana are considered the poorest and poverty levels are very high. Reliance to live is based solely upon growing crops, leading to malnutrition and poverty when droughts or other poor conditions destroy foods.
